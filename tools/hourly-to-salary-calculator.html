How to Convert Between Hourly Wages and Annual Salary

Whether you are evaluating a job offer, setting freelance rates, or simply trying to understand what your time is worth, converting between an hourly wage and an annual salary is one of the most practical calculations in personal finance. This tool handles the math instantly so you can focus on the decision rather than the arithmetic.

The Core Formula

The standard conversion relies on three numbers: your hourly rate, the number of hours you work each week, and the number of weeks you work each year. The formula is straightforward:

Annual Salary = Hourly Rate × Hours per Week × Weeks per Year

For a typical full-time employee working 40 hours per week for 52 weeks per year, that works out to 2,080 working hours annually. Multiply your hourly rate by 2,080 and you have your gross annual salary. To go the other direction — from salary to hourly — simply divide the annual salary by the total hours worked per year.

Factors That Affect the Conversion

The formula above gives you a clean gross figure, but real-world compensation is rarely that simple. Several factors can make the effective value of an hourly wage or salary higher or lower than the raw number suggests:

  • Paid time off (PTO) — Salaried employees often receive paid vacation, sick days, and holidays. If you take two weeks of paid vacation, you are still earning for 52 weeks while only working 50. An hourly worker who takes two weeks off unpaid effectively works fewer weeks, reducing annual income.
  • Benefits — Employer-provided health insurance, retirement contributions, and other perks add significant value on top of base salary. When comparing a salaried position with benefits to an hourly or freelance role without them, factor in the cost of purchasing those benefits independently.
  • Overtime — Many hourly workers earn time-and-a-half (1.5× their regular rate) for hours beyond 40 per week. Overtime pay can substantially increase actual annual earnings compared to the base rate calculation.
  • Taxes — Your tax bracket, filing status, and deductions all affect take-home pay. Two jobs with the same gross salary can produce different net incomes depending on your tax situation.

Common Use Cases

  • Job comparison — You receive one offer quoting an hourly rate and another quoting an annual salary. Converting both to the same basis lets you compare them fairly, especially after accounting for hours and benefits.
  • Freelance and contract pricing — Freelancers need to set hourly rates that cover not only equivalent salary but also self-employment taxes, insurance, and unpaid downtime. Start with your target annual income and work backward to find the hourly rate you need to charge.
  • Monthly budgeting — Knowing your monthly and biweekly income helps you set up a realistic household budget, plan savings contributions, and manage recurring expenses.
  • Salary negotiation — Understanding the hourly equivalent of a salary offer — or the annual value of an hourly rate — gives you concrete numbers to bring to a negotiation conversation.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much is $20 per hour annually?

At 40 hours per week for 52 weeks, $20 per hour equals $41,600 per year before taxes. If you work fewer weeks due to unpaid time off, multiply $20 by your actual hours per week and actual weeks worked to get a more accurate figure.

How do I calculate my hourly rate from a salary?

Divide your annual salary by the number of weeks you work per year, then divide that result by the number of hours you work per week. For example, a $60,000 salary at 40 hours per week for 52 weeks equals $60,000 ÷ 2,080 = approximately $28.85 per hour.

What about overtime — does this calculator account for it?

This calculator computes gross pay at a straight hourly rate. Overtime premiums (typically 1.5× or 2× your base rate for hours beyond 40 per week) depend on your employer, your employment classification, and local labor laws. To estimate overtime earnings, calculate your base salary using this tool and then add overtime pay separately.

Should I adjust weeks per year for unpaid time off?

Yes. If you are an hourly worker or contractor who does not receive paid vacation, reduce the weeks-per-year value to reflect the weeks you actually work. For example, if you take three weeks off unpaid, set weeks per year to 49 for a more accurate annual estimate.
