Understanding Currency Exchange Rates

Currency exchange rates determine how much one currency is worth in terms of another. Whether you are planning international travel, sending money abroad, pricing imported goods, or simply curious about global economics, understanding exchange rates gives you valuable financial literacy. This viewer provides approximate reference rates for 20 major world currencies against the U.S. dollar, allowing you to estimate conversions without relying on a live API or sharing any personal data.

How Exchange Rates Work

An exchange rate is essentially a price — the price of one currency expressed in units of another. When you see "1 USD = 0.92 EUR," it means one U.S. dollar buys 0.92 euros. Rates fluctuate constantly during trading hours based on supply and demand, interest rate differentials, inflation expectations, geopolitical events, and central bank policies. The rates shown in this tool are approximate mid-market rates — the midpoint between the buy and sell prices — and are intended for estimation purposes only.

Factors That Influence Exchange Rates

  • Interest rates — Countries with higher interest rates tend to attract more foreign investment, increasing demand for their currency and pushing its value up.
  • Inflation — Lower inflation generally strengthens a currency because its purchasing power erodes more slowly relative to other currencies.
  • Trade balance — Countries that export more than they import tend to see their currency appreciate due to foreign demand for their goods (and therefore their currency).
  • Political stability — Stable governments and predictable economic policies attract investment and strengthen the national currency.
  • Market speculation — Traders buying and selling currencies in large volumes can cause short-term fluctuations independent of economic fundamentals.

Common Use Cases

  • Travel budgeting — Estimate how much your home currency is worth at your destination so you can plan daily spending.
  • Online shopping — Convert prices on international websites to your local currency before purchasing.
  • Remittances — Get a rough idea of how much money will arrive when sending funds to family abroad, before fees and bank spreads.
  • Business invoicing — Quickly estimate costs when dealing with suppliers or clients in different countries.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why are bank rates different from mid-market rates?

Banks and currency exchange services add a markup — called the spread — on top of the mid-market rate. This spread is how they make money on the transaction. The mid-market rate (also called the interbank rate) is the "true" exchange rate between two currencies. When you exchange money at an airport kiosk, bank branch, or through a credit card transaction, you will receive a rate that is less favorable than the mid-market rate. The difference can range from 1% to 10% depending on the provider and location.

How can I get the best exchange rate?

Compare rates across multiple providers before exchanging. Online transfer services often offer better rates than traditional banks. Avoid airport currency exchange counters, which typically have the worst rates. Using a credit card with no foreign transaction fee is often the most cost-effective option for travel purchases. For large transfers, consider specialized foreign exchange brokers who offer rates closer to mid-market.

Are these rates live?

No. This tool uses hardcoded approximate rates for quick reference and estimation. Exchange rates change every second during trading hours. For live rates, use a dedicated financial data provider or your bank's conversion tool.
