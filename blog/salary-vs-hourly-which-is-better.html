Salary vs Hourly Pay: How to Compare (And What You Actually Take Home)

July 10, 2025 · 7 min read

When you're comparing job offers or evaluating your own compensation, comparing salary to hourly pay isn't as simple as multiplying an hourly rate by 2,080 hours. Benefits, overtime, taxes, and the hidden costs of each arrangement can shift the real value significantly. A $55,000 salary might actually be worth more than a $30/hour job — or less — depending on the specifics. In this guide, we'll show you exactly how to make an apples-to-apples comparison so you can make smarter career and financial decisions.

The Basic Conversion: Hourly to Annual

The standard conversion assumes 40 hours per week and 52 weeks per year:

Annual salary = Hourly rate × 40 hours × 52 weeks Annual salary = Hourly rate × 2,080

And the reverse:

Hourly equivalent = Annual salary ÷ 2,080

The Hidden Cost of Salary: Unpaid Overtime

Here's what many salaried employees don't realize: if you're working more than 40 hours a week, your effective hourly rate drops. A $65,000 salary at 40 hours/week works out to $31.25/hour. But if you're regularly working 50-hour weeks — common in management, tech, and professional roles — your effective rate drops to $25/hour.

When comparing a salary offer to an hourly position, be honest about how many hours the salaried role will actually require. Ask during the interview: "What does a typical week look like?" If the answer hints at 50+ hours, use that number in your calculation, not the theoretical 40.

The Hidden Benefit of Hourly: Overtime Pay

Under the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA), non-exempt hourly workers must be paid 1.5× their regular rate for hours over 40 per week. This is a significant advantage when overtime is available.

Consider a $25/hour worker who averages 5 hours of overtime per week:

Regular pay: $25 × 40 hrs × 52 weeks = $52,000 Overtime pay: $37.50 × 5 hrs × 52 weeks = $9,750 Total annual: $61,750

That $25/hour job with consistent overtime pays the equivalent of a $61,750 salary — nearly $10,000 more than the base conversion suggests. During busy periods with 10+ hours of weekly overtime, the difference is even larger.

Note: many salaried workers are classified as "exempt" from overtime under the FLSA if they earn above the salary threshold (currently $43,888/year as of 2024) and meet certain duties tests. This is why salaried managers often work 50+ hour weeks with no additional pay.

Factoring In Benefits: The Full Compensation Picture

Salary positions typically come with benefits that hourly positions may not. These benefits have real monetary value that should be part of your comparison:

401(k) employer match $2,000–$6,000 Typical 3%–6% match on a $65K salary

Dental & vision insurance $500–$1,500 Employer-paid portion

For a median salaried position at $65,000, benefits can add $15,000–$30,000 in total compensation value. This means a $65,000 salary with full benefits might be worth $80,000–$95,000 in total compensation. An hourly worker earning the same gross pay without benefits would need to purchase health insurance ($400–$600/month individually), save for their own retirement, and forgo paid time off — reducing their effective take-home significantly.

Self-Employment: A Different Calculation Entirely

Freelancers and independent contractors face a unique situation. If you're self-employed, you need to pay both the employee and employer portions of Social Security and Medicare taxes — an additional 7.65% on top of normal income tax. This is the self-employment tax.

Self-employment tax rate: 15.3% (12.4% Social Security + 2.9% Medicare) On $80,000 income: ~$11,304 in self-employment tax alone (Plus regular federal and state income taxes)

As a general rule, freelancers need to charge 30%–50% more than an equivalent employee hourly rate to end up with the same take-home pay after accounting for self-employment tax, self-funded health insurance, no paid time off, no employer retirement match, and business expenses.

If a full-time employee earns $40/hour ($83,200/year) with full benefits, a freelancer doing the same work needs to charge approximately $55–$60/hour to match the total compensation value.

How to Calculate Your True Hourly Rate

Whether you're salaried or hourly, knowing your true hourly rate helps you make better decisions about time. Here's the formula:

True hourly rate = (Annual salary + Benefits value − Work-related costs) ÷ Actual annual hours worked

For a $65,000 salaried worker with $18,000 in benefits, $3,000 in commuting/work clothing costs, working 47 hours/week for 50 weeks (2 weeks vacation):

True hourly rate = ($65,000 + $18,000 − $3,000) ÷ (47 × 50) True hourly rate = $80,000 ÷ 2,350 True hourly rate = $34.04/hr

Compare that to the nominal $31.25/hr ($65,000 ÷ 2,080). The benefits add value, but the extra hours and work costs modify the picture. Use our Hourly to Salary Calculator for quick conversions, and check our Tip Calculator if you're in an industry where tips supplement your hourly base.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is $25 an hour good pay?

At $25/hour working full-time, you'd earn $52,000 annually before taxes. Whether that's "good" depends entirely on your location and circumstances. In a low cost-of-living area (rural Midwest, parts of the South), $52,000 is a comfortable middle-class income. In San Francisco or New York City, it would be tight for a single person and very difficult for a family. The national median individual income is roughly $45,000, so $52,000 puts you above average nationwide. For context, $52,000 after federal and state taxes (assuming single, no dependents, in a moderate-tax state) leaves you about $40,000–$43,000 in take-home pay, or roughly $3,300–$3,600/month.

Can I negotiate switching from hourly to salary?

Yes, and it's common as you advance in your career. When negotiating, calculate the full compensation difference — not just the base pay. If you're earning $28/hour with 5 hours of regular overtime ($68,120/year gross), you'd want a salary offer of at least $70,000 with benefits to break even. If the salary offer eliminates overtime pay, factor those lost hours of premium pay into your counter. Present it as: "Based on my current total compensation including overtime, I'd need $X salary to make the transition work financially."

How do I know if I'm exempt or non-exempt from overtime?

Under the FLSA, you're generally exempt (not eligible for overtime) if you earn at least $43,888/year salary AND your primary duties involve executive, administrative, or professional work. Simply being paid a salary doesn't make you exempt — the duties test matters too. Many employers misclassify employees to avoid paying overtime. If you're unsure, the Department of Labor has a detailed duties test on their website, or consult an employment attorney. Being misclassified could mean you're owed back overtime pay.

Should I take a pay cut to go from hourly to salary?

It depends on the full picture. A small pay cut (5%–10%) might be worth it if the salaried position offers substantially better benefits (health insurance alone can be worth $8,000–$16,000/year), career advancement opportunities, more predictable income, or paid time off. Use our Profit Margin Calculator to evaluate side business income if you're comparing employment to self-employment scenarios. Calculate total compensation for both options before deciding.

How do taxes differ between salary and hourly?

Federal income taxes are the same for both — what matters is your total annual income, not how you're paid. The key difference is in withholding: salaried employees have consistent tax withholding each paycheck, while hourly workers' withholding fluctuates with hours. Hourly workers with variable schedules are more likely to owe money at tax time (if withholding was based on a light week) or get a large refund (if based on a heavy week). Either way, the actual tax owed at year-end is calculated the same way for both.
